Job Market Paper

The Aggregate and Welfare Effects of Completing Financial Markets in Agriculture [Paper]

This paper investigates the effects of completing financial markets in a poor agrarian-based economy: rural Uganda. To do so, I build a heterogeneous-agent incomplete markets model with endogenous agricultural income risk. Under the model, low-wealth households give up profitability to reduce risk exposure: they under-invest in inputs and shift inputs usage to less productive and safer crops. I calibrate the model using the Ugandan National Panel Survey and compare it to a counterfactual economy with complete markets in agriculture. Quantitatively, I find the following results. First, completing financial markets increases agricultural production by 29 percent. It increases inputs usage and leads to a more efficient allocation of inputs within households ---across crops--- and across households. Second, costly precautionary savings decrease. The two effects lead to significant gains in consumption across the entire distribution while consumption fluctuations strongly decrease. Altogether, completing financial markets in agriculture generates a welfare gain of 32 percent in consumption terms.

Progessivity in the Village
With Francesco Carli, and Raül Santaeulàlia-Llopis.

Description: Using primary data on food transfers for a complete village in rural Malawi, we find that the extent of transfer progressivity across households is large---with an income-to-transfer elasticity of 0.60. The solution to village-calibrated versions of endogenously incomplete markets models arising from limited commitment (LC) and private information (PI) imply constrained-efficient levels of progressivity that are substantially lower than the actual levels of progressivity in the village. The introduction of costly-state verification takes the constrained-efficient transfers progressivity closer to complete markets. Yet, the first best implies a progressivity level that is still lower than that of the village. To rationalize the actual allocations we introduce social norms (wedges) in the LC and PI constraints and single out the quantitatively role of the ex-ante participation constraint. Interestingly, these wedges tend to disappear if we decrease current village productivity to past productivity levels---e.g. a pre-fertilizers era. Our results suggest that the currently inefficient excess of progressivity can be the result of social norms or traditions that pursue high social insurance (and redistribution) and that are sluggish to adapt to economic change.

Eating Up Productivity: Social Insurance Barriers to Economic Growth
With Raül Santaeulàlia-Llopis.

Description: In poor countries, the provision of social insurance (SI) is large---and associated with a large degree of transfer progressivity---whereas the macroeconomic performance is characterized by low factor accumulation, low total factor productivity (TFP) and slow structural change. We propose an OLG heterogeneous agent structural change (HAST) model where agents exogenously differ in age (cohort) and productivity (permanent and transitory) and endogenously choose education, asset portfolio (land and capital) and area of residency. Rural and urban areas differ in their production goods, their production technology, the nature and size of risk, and the availability of SI mechanisms. In rural areas, self-employed households use a risky technology to produce agricultural goods and SI is provided through a transfer scheme that is progressive. In urban areas, agents work in the non-agricultural goods sector, face labor income risk and can only self-insure. We calibrate our model economy using micro panel data for Malawi. In status quo, our model aims to replicate the distributions of consumption, income and wealth within urban and rural areas, the extent of factor input misallocation within rural areas and a set of migration (negative selection) patterns. Our preliminary results show that the optimal transfer progressivity is smaller than the status quo. The lower optimal progressivity unravels a process of structural change that increases factor accumulation and aggregate TFP. The improvement of TFP occurs through a better allocation of resources across rural and urban areas---migration rates increase, in particular, for those highly productive in destination---and a better allocation of resources within rural areas. We will study further policies including education subsidies that can reduce the need for transfer progressivity (\`a la Krueger and Ludwig, 2016) and policy instruments in urban areas that can potentially cross-subsidize insurance in rural areas.

So Far Away from You: Market Access and Welfare
With Bruno Conte.

Description: The objective of this paper is to study the effects of market access on three main welfare outcomes: growth, inequality, and consumption risk. We use household panel data from Uganda with detailed information on household’s consumption, income and wealth; and their distance to main roads, markets and town centers. Our preliminary empirical findings suggest that market access has a strong positive association with growth, between regions inequality and income risk, but no relationship with consumption risk. We find that a toy model based on an Aiyagari economy with a discrete choice on accessing markets can account for these trends. To further develop the study, we plan to use official road data and work with a fully-fledge macroeconomic model on spatial-trade and incomplete markets.

Network Formation in a Village Economy
With Leandro de Magalhaes, Pau Milán, and Raül Santaeulàlia-Llopis.

Description: We study the formation of the food transfers network and relational networks using primary data on a complete village of Southern Malawi. We find that a substantial number of inter-household transfers occur outside the relational networks, i.e., kinship, friendship, and worshipping. The relational networks are homophilic, thus presenting some potential for self-sustained risk-sharing equilibria but little potential for a progressive redistributive system. Once we look at actual transfers within the relational networks, the evidence suggests a progressive redistributional transfers system. Altruism can rationalize redistributional transfers that occur within the relational network. However we find strong evidence of a progressive redistributive system outside the relational networks. Transfers occur across social groups, sub-villages, religion, income, wealth, and age. Such transfers may be sustained either by village-wide altruism, by social norms and tradition, or by a more complex informational risk-sharing network.
