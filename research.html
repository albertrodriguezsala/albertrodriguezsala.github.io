Working Papers


Climate Change and Migration (job market paper)
(draft available soon)

This paper uses subnational population, GDP and agricultural data for 43 countries of sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), together with forecasts of agricultural productivity changes in a climate change scenario, to quantify potential migration flows induced by climate change. I take general equilibrium effects into consideration by building a static, multi-sector spatial trade model in which agglomeration and congestion forces shape the economic activity on the geography. I estimate the model to fit the SSA economy of 2000 to then solve for 2080's optimal spatial distribution of the economic activity and population. My results suggest substantial population displacement, of several million people, and mostly into the largest urban centers. I also find that some countries could experience a manifold increase in population if compared to a scenario with barriers to migration between countries. The GDP losses associated with climate change, in that scenario, are estimated to be up to 3 times larger, for some countries, than the losses in a scenario with no country barriers.


Ancient Plagues in Modern Times: The Impact of Desert Locust Invasions on Child Health
joint with L. Piemontese and A. Tapsoba, new draft coming soon!.

This paper estimates the causal impact on child health of a locust plague that occurred in Mali during the mid 2000s. Using a Difference-in-Differences strategy, we show that children who were exposed in utero to the plague suffer major health setbacks. Affected children have, on average, a height-for-age Z-score 0.39 points lower than non-exposed children. We argue that, in this type of agricultural economy, locust invasions could have an impact on child health mainly through two channels: first, a speculative/anticipatory price effect that kicks in during the plague itself, followed by local crop failures effect that would constitute an income shock for affected farmers and a local food supply shock for markets. We find that children exposed only to the speculative price effect in utero suffer as much as those exposed to the actual crop failure effect.
