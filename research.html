Working Papers


Climate Change and Migration (job market paper)
(draft available soon)

This paper uses subnational population, GDP, and agricultural data for 42 countries of sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), together with forecasts for agricultural productivity changes in a climate change scenario, to quantify potential migration flows induced by climate change. I take a general equilibrium approach by building a multi-sector spatial trade model in which agglomeration and congestion forces shape the economic activity on the geography. I estimate the model to fit the SSA economy of 2000 to then solve for 2080’s optimal spatial distribution of the economic activity and population. My results suggest substantial population displacement, of about 12 percent of SSA’s population by 2080. Moreover, a simulation with country migration barriers shows that migration flows would be likewise large, driven by internal migration, but GDP losses up to 4 times larger. Finally, I find that technological adoption could reverse considerably the negative impacts of climate change.


The Power of Markets: Impact of Desert Locust Invasions on Child Health
joint with L. Piemontese and A. Tapsoba, submitted.

This paper investigates the consequences of a locust plague that occurred in Mali in 2004. We provide evidence of substantial crop market effects that explain the space-time pattern of the estimated impact of this plague. We argue first that the plague has affected households in Mali through two channels: first, a speculative price effect that kicked in during the plague itself, followed by a local crop failure effect. We find that, in terms of health setbacks, children exposed in utero only to the speculative price effect suffered as much as those exposed to the actual crop failure effect. Once we account for the impact on local crop prices, the estimated speculative effect vanishes, whereas the crop failure effect persists. Children born in isolated areas suffer more from the crop failure effect. Our results suggest that addressing local market reactions to this type of agricultural shocks is crucial for policy design.


The Changing Economic Geography of a Warming World
joint with K. Desmet, D. Nagy, and E. Rossi-Hansberg (draft available soon)

This paper quantitatively analyzes the world’s changing economic geography due to global warming. It proposes a two-sector dynamic spatial growth model that incorporates the relation between economic activity, carbon emissions and temperature. To analyze the impact of global warming, it takes the model to the data at the 1 by 1 degrees resolution for the entire world. Taking a 200-year horizon, rising temperatures have an important effect on where people will live and where agriculture will thrive. Migration and trade play an essential role in the reshaping of the world’s economic geography due to climate change.
