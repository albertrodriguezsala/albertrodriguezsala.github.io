Working Papers

The Impact of Incomplete Financial Markets on Crop-Choice, Agricultural Productivity, and Welfare [Paper]

I study the aggregate and welfare impact of completing financial markets in a poor agrarian-based economy, rural Uganda. Using micro-data from the Ugandan National Panel Survey (UNPS), I document that (1) farmers face a trade-off between crop yields and crop risk, (2) low-yield low-risk crops represent the main share of output for poor farmers, and (3) most farmers grow both types of crops. Based on this evidence, I build a heterogeneous-agent model with a crop-portfolio investment choice. Under the model, low-wealth households optimally give up profitability to reduce risk exposure: they under-invest in intermediate inputs and shift inputs usage from a high-risk high-returns technology---high-yield crops---to a low-risk low-returns technology---low-yield crops. I calibrate the model to key moments in agriculture, risk, income, and wealth with the UNPS data. Despite that households in rural Uganda have large levels of consumption smoothing, I find that the gains in completing financial markets in agriculture are large. Agricultural productivity increases by 21.75 percent, with almost half of this gain coming directly from a reallocation of inputs across farmers and crops. In terms of consumption and welfare, the gains are even larger with a 25 percent increase in average consumption and a welfare gain equal to 28.4 percent.


Do the Poor Insure their Consumption Better? Empirical Evidence from Uganda [Paper]

Using nationally representative panel household data, I study the difference in consumption insurance levels across the quintiles of the consumption, income, and wealth distributions in Uganda. I find that poor households do insure their consumption better than rich households. Under several econometric specifications, consumption insurance coefficients are notably---and in many cases statistically significantly---lower for the households in the bottom quintiles of the consumption and wealth distributions than for the households in the top quintiles. The results are driven by rural areas where most Ugandan households reside. In urban areas, the relationship between insurance and economic levels is the opposite: rich households experience much larger levels of insurance than poor households. Overall, the results suggests there is trade-off between insurance and economic levels across households.
